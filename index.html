01 — The Big Idea

What is a DCF?

A Discounted Cash Flow is a way to answer one simple question: "If I add up all the money this business will make in the future, what is that worth in today's dollars?"

The word "discounted" is the key. A dollar earned ten years from now is worth less than a dollar today — because you could invest today's dollar and grow it. So we shrink each future dollar to reflect what it would be worth right now.

Plain English

If someone offered you $100 today or $100 in five years, you'd take the money today. A DCF calculates exactly how much less that future $100 is worth — then adds up all those shrunken amounts to arrive at a total value.

1

Estimate future revenue

How much money will Starlink bring in each year? We start with what it earned this year and project growth forward.

2

Calculate Operating Profit

Not all revenue is profit. After paying for satellites, launches, and daily operations, what's left? That's Operating Profit — the money the business actually keeps before interest and taxes.

3

Discount it back to today

Each year's profit gets shrunk by a "discount rate" — a percentage that accounts for risk and what you could earn elsewhere. We used 10%.

4

Add a Terminal Value

The business won't stop after ten years. The terminal value estimates what all profits beyond year ten are worth — assuming slow, steady growth forever after.

5

Add it all up

Sum of discounted profits + terminal value = what Starlink is worth today.

About 71% of the value comes from the terminal component. That's typical — it represents the market's bet on Starlink's long-term earning power, not just the next decade.

Analogy

Think of buying an apartment building. The first ten years of rent are valuable, but most of the building's price comes from the fact that it will keep collecting rent for decades. The terminal value captures that "forever" earning power.

06 — What Could Go Wrong

Reasons this might be too high — or too low

↓

Growth could stall

Sustaining 50% growth for five years is extremely aggressive. Competition from Amazon's Kuiper, regulatory barriers in major markets, or slower adoption could trim the trajectory.

↓

Satellites have expiry dates

Starlink satellites burn up after roughly five years. The company must perpetually build and launch replacements — a permanent cost that caps how high margins can climb.

↑

Starship changes everything

SpaceX's next-generation rocket could reduce launch costs by tenfold, making satellite replacement dramatically cheaper and pushing margins higher than this model assumes.

↑

Direct-to-cell is a wildcard

Starlink has begun beaming internet directly to ordinary smartphones — no dish required. If this scales, the addressable market could be far larger than $300 billion.
